## Question 1
What is the primary purpose of the Sampling or Modeling Approach in cost segregation?

A) To provide estimates when no actual cost data exists
B) To analyze multiple properties that are nearly identical by studying a representative sample and applying results to similar properties
C) To randomly select properties from a portfolio for individual detailed studies
D) To create theoretical models without actual property analysis

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: B ✓
**Explanation**: The study material states: "Uses a created model or template to analyze multiple properties that are nearly identical in construction and use. This approach leverages economies of scale by studying a representative sample and applying results to similar properties." This method is specifically designed for situations where multiple similar properties exist—such as fast-food chains or retail outlets with prototype designs—allowing for efficient analysis by studying a sample and applying those results across the portfolio.

## Question 4

A) Apply industry-standard percentages to all 150 properties immediately
B) Identify a representative sample of properties, ensure it captures typical variations, document selection criteria, and verify sample size is statistically appropriate
C) Perform detailed studies on all 150 locations simultaneously
D) Use the residual estimation approach for the entire portfolio

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: B ✓
**Explanation**: The study material outlines a four-step process, with "Step 1: Sample Selection" requiring exactly what option B describes: "Identify a representative sample of properties, Ensure sample captures typical variations, Document selection criteria, [and] Verify sample size is statistically appropriate." You don't jump directly to applying percentages (A) without first developing a model from sample analysis. Studying all 150 locations (C) defeats the efficiency purpose of the sampling approach. And using residual estimation (D) is a different methodology entirely. The proper first step is careful, documented sample selection.

## Question 6

A) Verifying similarity to model specifications
B) Documenting any significant variations
C) Obtaining new construction cost estimates for each property
D) Reconciling to actual costs for each property

**Your Answer**: C
**Correct Answer**: C ✓
**Explanation**: Step 4 states that model results (percentages) are "applied to the entire population after: Verifying similarity to model specifications, Documenting any significant variations, Adjusting for location and timing differences, [and] Reconciling to actual costs for each property." The approach applies the percentage allocations developed from the sample to the actual costs of each remaining property. Obtaining new construction cost estimates (C) is not required or mentioned—the model leverages actual historical costs and applies the sample-derived percentages to those costs, not to new estimates.

## Question 8
A franchise organization is choosing between performing detailed individual studies on all 200 locations versus using the Sampling or Modeling Approach. The properties were all built to identical specifications over a 2-year period in similar geographic regions. How should they evaluate this decision from a professional standards perspective?

A) Always choose individual studies regardless of similarity to maximize accuracy
B) Consider that sampling/modeling is cost-effective for similar properties while maintaining consistency, but requires truly similar properties, thorough sample analysis, variation documentation, and individual reconciliation
C) Use sampling/modeling for all properties automatically since they're franchises
D) Randomly alternate between detailed and sampling approaches across the portfolio

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: B ✓
**Explanation**: This synthesis question tests professional judgment in methodology selection. The study material shows sampling/modeling offers "cost-effective for multiple similar properties," "maintains consistency," and is "scalable to large property counts"—all advantages for 200 similar locations. However, it also requires professional rigor: properties must be "truly similar," the approach "requires detailed work" on the sample, "must account for variations," and the IRS requires that "variations are documented" and "individual property costs reconcile to actual totals." Option B reflects this balanced analysis. Options A and C represent inappropriate absolute rules, while D lacks any professional rationale.
