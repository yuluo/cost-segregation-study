## Question 3
What two methods does the study material identify for allocating soft costs to property categories?

A) Time-based allocation and square-footage allocation
B) Direct allocation (when possible) and pro-rata allocation based on hard cost percentages
C) Engineer-assigned allocation and IRS-prescribed allocation tables
D) Single-category allocation and cost-rank-based allocation

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: B ✓
**Explanation**: The study material identifies two allocation methods: (1) Direct allocation — used "when possible," such as engineering fees allocated to specific systems designed, permits to eligible property components, and interest based on construction timeline; and (2) Pro-rata allocation — used "when direct allocation isn't feasible," distributing costs based on hard cost percentages proportionally across all property classes. Direct allocation is preferred as it maximizes accuracy.

D) Increase the cost by a standard 10% historical adjustment factor

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: B ✓

## Question 5
A cost segregation study is being performed for a warehouse in San Francisco. The national average published cost for concrete flooring is $8.00/SF. The San Francisco location factor is 1.35. What is the correctly adjusted cost?

A) $8.00/SF — location factors apply only to labor, not materials
B) $9.35/SF — add the location factor to the base cost
C) $10.80/SF — multiply the base cost by the location factor
D) $6.67/SF — divide the base cost by the location factor

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: C ✗
**Explanation**: The study material's example shows location factors are applied by multiplication: "National average cost: $100/SF, San Francisco location factor: 1.35" — the adjusted cost would be $100 × 1.35 = $135/SF. Applying the same logic: $8.00 × 1.35 = $10.80/SF. Option B ($9.35) incorrectly adds the factor to the base cost rather than multiplying. The location factor is a multiplier that scales the national average to reflect local market conditions — in San Francisco's high-cost market, the 1.35 factor reflects costs 35% above the national average.

D) All component quantities should be increased proportionally to reach $5 million

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: B ✓
**Explanation**: The study material's reconciliation formula shows: "Sum of all component costs + Allocated soft costs + Contractor markup (if not in unit costs) = Total estimated/actual project cost." The $800,000 gap is a classic sign that soft costs and contractor markup haven't been added yet. The study material lists contractor markup components as general conditions (5-10%), overhead (5-15%), profit (5-15%), and bond/insurance (1-3%) — these are applied on top of direct costs.

## Question 9
Two identical office buildings were built in the same year — one in downtown New York (location factor 1.40) and one in rural Iowa (location factor 0.75). A junior analyst proposes using a single blended factor of 1.075 for both to save time. The published base cost is $200/SF. What problem does this create?

A) No problem — blending factors is an acceptable time-saving approach for similar property types
B) The blended approach systematically distorts both allocations: New York should be $280/SF but gets $215/SF (understated by $65), Iowa should be $150/SF but gets $215/SF (overstated by $65) — creating inaccurate cost allocations across recovery periods that couldn't withstand IRS scrutiny
C) The problem only arises if the two properties file separate tax returns
D) This is only acceptable if both properties were acquired in the same transaction

**Your Answer**: B
**Correct Answer**: B ✓
**Explanation**: The study material shows that location factors create real, material differences: San Francisco at 1.35, New York at 1.40, and rural Midwest at 0.85. Applying the math: New York at $200 × 1.40 = $280/SF vs. blended $200 × 1.075 = $215/SF (understated by $65/SF); Iowa at $200 × 0.75 = $150/SF vs. blended $215/SF (overstated by $65/SF). These errors flow directly into personal property vs. real property allocations and resulting depreciation deductions.
