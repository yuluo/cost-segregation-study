## Question 1
What is the primary basis for the "Rule of Thumb" approach in cost segregation?

A) Detailed engineering analysis of specific building components
B) Published cost data from recognized industry sources like R.S. Means
C) Generalized percentages based on a preparer's experience or industry averages, without project-specific documentation
D) Statistical sampling of a representative subset of properties in a portfolio

**Your Answer**: C
**Correct Answer**: C ✓
**Explanation**: The study material defines the Rule of Thumb approach as "based on a preparer's 'experience' or industry averages rather than project-specific documentation," applying "generalized percentages without detailed analysis of the specific property." This is fundamentally different from methodologies that use actual construction records, published cost databases, or representative sampling — all of which involve project-specific analysis.

## Question 2
According to the study material, what is the IRS's general position on the Rule of Thumb approach?

A) It is the preferred method for smaller properties under $1 million in value
B) It is acceptable when the preparer documents their industry averages thoroughly
C) It views this method with extreme caution and generally does not accept it as sufficient support for cost segregation allocations
D) It is acceptable when the preparer has 10 or more years of cost segregation experience

**Your Answer**: C
**Correct Answer**: C ✓
**Explanation**: The study material states clearly: "The IRS views this method with extreme caution and generally does not accept it as sufficient support for cost segregation allocations." The IRS concerns include: lacks sufficient documentation, does not account for property-specific characteristics, no verification of actual construction components, impossible to reconcile to actual project costs, and cannot be independently verified or reproduced. Neither the property's size (A), documentation of averages (B), nor the preparer's experience level (D) changes this fundamental IRS position.

**Correct Answer**: C ✗

## Question 8
A client with a $500,000 retail renovation has custom fixtures and specialized lighting throughout. They ask their advisor to use the Rule of Thumb approach ("retail is 20-25% personal property") to save $3,000 in study costs. What should the advisor recommend, and why?

A) Proceed with Rule of Thumb — the $3,000 savings clearly justifies the approach
B) Use Rule of Thumb but increase the percentage to 35% to account for the custom work
C) Recommend a detailed engineering or cost estimate approach — the custom fixtures and specialized lighting likely differ materially from generic retail averages, creating audit risk and potential disallowance that far outweighs the $3,000 savings
D) Use Rule of Thumb for the first year, then perform a detailed study when the property is placed in service

**Your Answer**: C
**Correct Answer**: C ✓
**Explanation**: This scenario illustrates a core principle from the study material: property-specific features like "tenant improvements and customization" and "technological systems installed" make rule-of-thumb averages unreliable. Custom fixtures and specialized lighting are exactly the components that drive allocations away from generic retail averages. The study material warns of "potential disallowance of entire study" as an audit risk — if the IRS challenges and disallows the entire study, the client loses far more than $3,000 in savings. Arbitrarily inflating the percentage (B) has no analytical basis and doesn't solve the documentation problem. The advisor's professional duty is to recommend an appropriate methodology, not to optimize for short-term cost savings.
